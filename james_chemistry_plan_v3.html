04 · Method
How H1 answers are built
Specific habits derived from what the LC Chemistry marking scheme rewards. Each one is used from Week 1 — not introduced gradually.
The three-step read — every calculation, every time
Write these three lines at the top of every calculation before writing a single number. 30 seconds. Prevents the most common error: wrong starting value.
Given:
Everything the question provides, with units already converted. "V(HCl) = 25 cm³ = 0.025 L, c(HCl) = 0.1 mol/L"
Finding:
What you're being asked for. "Finding: concentration of NaOH in mol/L." Forces correct question reading before starting.
Formula:
Write the formula before substituting. "n = c × v." Then substitute. Then calculate. The scheme awards marks for correct formula selection — showing it explicitly never loses marks and often gains them.
Show every step — show every unit
Marks are awarded at each step, not just the final answer
A 6-mark calculation has ~3 steps worth 2 marks each. Wrong final answer + correct method shown = 4/6. Correct final answer + no working = 0–2/6. Always show working — this is the single highest-yield habit in LC Chemistry.
Units at every line — always
"n = 0.1 × 0.025 = 0.0025" risks losing marks. "n = 0.1 mol/L × 0.025 L = 0.0025 mol" never does. Write units through every step without exception.
The learning log — one sentence after every question
Format: "The scheme rewarded [X] which I missed because [Y]"
Example: "The scheme rewarded stating the colour change 'persists for 30 seconds' — I wrote 'permanent colour change' without the time condition." Keep every sentence in one dedicated notebook page or Todoist note. By Week 10 this is your September handoff document.
Categorise each entry: theory / calculation / procedural language
Theory loss → the Grinds360 session needed more depth. Calculation loss → three-step read not applied strictly enough. Procedural language loss → add that experiment to write-from-memory practice. The category tells you exactly what to fix.
If Wednesday feels shaky — the Friday rule
Don't go straight to the past paper if the theory isn't solid
If Wednesday's Grinds360 session left you uncertain on the chapter — spend the first 10 minutes of Friday re-reading the key points before attempting the question. A past paper attempt on shaky foundations produces bad habits, not learning. The 10-minute re-read is not optional on those weeks.

Fixed-mark questions — bank them
Mandatory experiments appear on almost every LC Chemistry paper. Procedure questions are worth 6–8 marks each and reward exact procedural language. Learn once, bank permanently. ExperimentChapter · Grinds360 refFrequencyThe exact language to learn
Acid-base titration Ch17 · G360 Ch10 Every year Rinse burette with the acid solution. Fill burette, remove air bubble from tip, record initial reading. Using a pipette, transfer the known volume of base to the conical flask. Add 2–3 drops of indicator. Run acid from burette slowly — dropwise near endpoint — until the indicator colour change persists for 30 seconds. Record final burette reading. Repeat until two concordant results obtained (within 0.1 cm³).
Preparation of a standard solution Ch14 · G360 Ch7 Every year Accurately weigh the required mass of solid on a balance. Dissolve in a small volume of distilled water in a beaker with stirring. Transfer quantitatively to a volumetric flask. Rinse the beaker three times with distilled water, adding each rinsing to the flask. Make up to the graduation mark with distilled water. Stopper and invert several times to mix.
Rate of reaction (sodium thiosulphate + HCl) Ch15 · G360 Ch13 Most years Measure time from mixing reactants until the cross drawn on paper beneath the flask is no longer visible through the solution. Change one variable only (temperature / concentration). Plot rate (1/time) on y-axis vs the independent variable on x-axis. State conclusion from the graph trend.
Redox titration (iron(II) vs KMnO₄) Ch19 · G360 Ch12 Most years Same procedure as acid-base titration but: KMnO₄ is self-indicating — no external indicator needed. Endpoint = first permanent pink/purple colour that persists for 30 seconds. KMnO₄ goes in the burette. Acidify the iron(II) solution with dilute H₂SO₄ before titrating.
Electroplating Ch20 · school textbook Most years Cathode = object to be plated (clean before use). Anode = the plating metal. Electrolyte = solution of the plating metal's salt. Connect to DC power supply. Current flows: metal dissolves at anode, deposits at cathode. Mass increase at cathode = mass of metal deposited.

07 · AAC project
Scheduling — not planning
Full project planning is in the dedicated Chemistry project chat. This section covers only where project time fits in the summer schedule and which study chapters are simultaneously building project skills.
The Chemistry AAC project is 40% of your LC Chemistry grade. A perfect written exam gives you 60% maximum without it. It is not supplementary — it is half the subject.
Where project time sits in the summer schedule
Friday — last 20 min — alternating weeks
Chemistry AAC one Friday, Physics AAC the next. Brief and consistent — background reading, research question refinement, equipment thinking. Across 10 weeks this is ~2 hours of focused project work. Enough to arrive in September with real foundations.
20 min / fortnight
Sunday — optional, 30 min max
If a project task needs more time (reading guidelines in full, drafting the research question), Sunday after Anki is the slot. Keep it to 30 minutes — Sunday is a recovery day.
30 min optional
Study chapters doing double duty as project preparation
Week 1 — Ch17 Titration technique
Your project is a titration investigation. Understanding the procedure, equipment, and calculation method at exam level means your project methodology is grounded in correct chemistry from day one.
Week 7 — Ch14 Solutions & Concentration
Preparing solutions of known concentration is a core project skill. The standard solution preparation you learn for the exam is exactly what you need to set up your project titrations.
Week 9 — Ch18 pH & Buffer chemistry
Sodium bicarbonate is a buffer. Ka, Kw, and acid-base equilibria are the theoretical foundation of your investigation. This chapter is simultaneously your project's background chemistry section.
Week 5 — Ch16 Acids & Bases theory
The chemistry of neutralisation and acid strength underpins the written rationale for why your experimental design works. Conjugate pairs are directly relevant to buffer action.
